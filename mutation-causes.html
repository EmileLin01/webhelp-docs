Causes of Mutation

Suitable hosts infected by the Voidborne virus become mutants.

Mutations are caused by the Voidborne virus â€” a pathogen of extraterrestrial origin that latches onto and infects any creature large enough to sustain it. It arrived on Earth aboard the 2-kilometer-wide asteroid that struck Cajamarca, Peru, in 2038. The resulting shockwave dispersed an estimated 126,000 tons of the pathogen across the globe, triggering an outbreak so devastating that made COVID-19 look like child's play in comparison. Within weeks, any creature larger than a house cat that had been significantly exposed began to show signs of mutation. At first, the symptoms resembled those from rabies: foaming at the mouth, hypersensitivity to light and sound, and uncontrollable aggression. But instead of dying, affected organisms grew rapidly in size and strength, with thickened muscle fibers, denser bones, and accelerated metabolism. Above all, they developed cognitive functions related to hunting with a level of sophistication previously unseen in the animal kingdom, making even the smallest of these creatures extremely lethal.

Tragically, humans were not immune to the outbreak. Rural populations and anyone who spent prolonged time outdoors stood little chance of escaping infection. One year after the Impact, it was estimated that approximately 4.4 billion people had been infected by the Voidborne virus. While the vast majority have since been (regrettably) neutralized, the descendants of the survivors are more dangerous than ever. The latest generation of hosts have been observed leaping over 12 meters, tearing apart armored vehicles with their claws, and withstanding explosions from rocket-propelled shells.

Note:
Once the Voidborne virus enters a host organism, it binds to neural and endocrine receptors, hijacking the host's hormonal regulation and cellular repair mechanisms. The virus integrates into the host's genome, but does not replicate outside of a living body, which prevents it from being transmissible between hosts.
