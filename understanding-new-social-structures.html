Understanding New Social Structures

Modern civilization has broken down; survivors have reverted to tribalism, bartering, and in some cases, nomadism.

After arriving on Earth, the Voidborne virus rapidly spread across countless species worldwide, giving rise to tens of billions of apex predators in a matter of weeks. Blindsided, governments stood no chance at stimying the ensuing carnage, and one fell after another. Remote settlements, spared by the initial devastation, became humanityâ€™s last surviving outposts. By 2040, 95% of the global population had been wiped out, with the remaining survivors likely holed inside these makeshift strongholds.

Return to Tribalism

Individual settlements and colonies now serve as the highest form of governance. Ruling these bases are often strongmen, charismatic militants who can command the loyalty of what warriors and soldiers who remain. With trade between enclaves nearly nonexistent, most have become entirely self-sufficient. Resources remain scarce across the board, however, so most of these communities are distrustful of outsiders and quick to exile anybody who threatens their stability. Disputes over steel, the single most valuable commodity in the post-apocalyptic world, have further strained relations between the remaining human factions.

Barter System

With no central authority to uphold its value, money has become completely uselessâ€”replaced by an archaic barter system. While food, clothes, medicine, and weaponry have all contributed in usurping its prominence, steel is the true commodity that rendered money obselete. Steel can single-handedly determine whether a colony survives or perishes. Beyond crafting weapons and tools, it is essential for building walls, vehicles, and the machinery that keeps a settlement humming along. Individuals who can find sources of steel are always handsomely rewarded by the leaders of their community.

Nomadic Gangs

Not all humans dwell behind fortified walls; some, cast out by their communities or by choice, eke out a living in the mutant-infested wilds. Most, of course, do not live long, but those who do survive develop skills that set them far apart from their sedentary counterparts. Experts in tracking and mutant-hunting, nomads are a key component of any human endeavor against the mutant threat. As such, the relation between settlements and nomads is a strained one: the former wants nothing to do with them but also do without their aid. Nomadic leaders understand this dynamic all too well, and some have gained immense power and influence as a result.
